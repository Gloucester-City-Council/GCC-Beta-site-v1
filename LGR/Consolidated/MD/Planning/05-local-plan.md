# The Local Plan

<!-- common -->
This is the one part of planning content that's genuinely, structurally local — not a case of the same rule with a different number attached.

By law, every council must produce a Local Plan, covering a minimum 15-year horizon, setting out how development — housing, business, infrastructure — will be delivered in that area. Government sets the national housing delivery targets; the Local Plan is each council's own decision about how its share is best met given local circumstances, land availability, and priorities, developed with resident input through public consultation. Without an up-to-date Local Plan, a council has much less ability to resist or shape speculative development — and government can ultimately intervene and write the plan itself if a council fails to keep one current, removing local control over what gets built.

Because the plan-making process, evidence base structure (Strategic Housing Market Assessments, housing trajectories, brownfield land registers, Gypsy and Traveller accommodation assessments) and monitoring cycle (Housing Delivery Test, Five Year Housing Land Supply) are broadly standardised in method across councils, this is genuinely a case where the *process* is common but the *content and numbers* are entirely local.

<!-- council:forest-of-dean -->
**Forest of Dean District Council.** Required by national government to allow a minimum of 372 new homes per year, totalling 7,440 new homes by 2041. The council has no discretion over the overall number, but does retain influence over type and location. The current Local Plan is structured around six objectives: building a stronger local economy; regenerating town centres and bringing unused sites back into use; meeting local housing needs (in keeping with the character of the Forest); tackling climate change; improving and protecting the environment; and promoting healthy, active travel choices. The Allocations Plan (adopted June 2018) sits alongside the Core Strategy, updating the housing requirement and setting settlement boundaries and site allocations; it went through a legal challenge in 2013 (successfully defended) relating to the Cinderford Northern Quarter Area Action Plan, adopted 23 February 2012 as part of the wider regeneration strategy for that area.
