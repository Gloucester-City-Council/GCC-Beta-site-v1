# How a decision is made

<!-- common -->
Once your application is validated, you'll get an acknowledgement giving a target decision date, the name of the assigned planning officer, and your application reference.

## Consultation

Neighbours likely to be affected are notified and given the chance to comment — usually a minimum of 21 days from the date on the site notice or neighbour letter. A site or press notice may also go up where planning law requires it (for example: applications affecting a listed building or its setting, a conservation area, an environmental impact assessment, a departure from the development plan, or a public right of way). Technical consultees — Highways, Historic England, the Environment Agency, the water company, and others depending on the application — may be asked to comment. Anyone can view and comment on a live application through the council's public access system; you don't need to register, though registering lets you track applications and get email alerts. Comments (not your contact details) are published.

## Assessment

The planning officer weighs the application against: Local Plan policy, national planning policy and guidance, the site's planning history, any likely detrimental impact on neighbours (loss of light, privacy, or outlook, for example), and comments received from consultees and neighbours. They may request further information or amendments before making a recommendation.

## Who decides

Most applications are decided under delegated authority by an officer. Applications that are more significant, contentious, or don't fit clearly within policy are referred to the planning committee, and you'll be told if that applies to yours — with an opportunity to speak.

## Timescales

Set nationally, not by the council:
- **8 weeks** — householder and non-major applications
- **13 weeks** — major applications
- **16 weeks** — major applications requiring an Environmental Impact Assessment

These are targets, not guarantees. If the council needs amendments to address concerns, an extension of time is usually agreed, and it becomes unlikely the original target will be met.

## Duration of permission

In most cases you have 3 years from the decision to start development, after which permission lapses. Always check the specific conditions attached to your permission — some conditions need discharging before work can start at all, and later changes to your plans are likely to need a further application.

<!-- council:forest-of-dean -->
**Forest of Dean District Council.** Statutory publicity duties are documented in detail: a green site notice is posted for the categories of application planning law requires (as above), plus a statutory newspaper advertisement for the same categories. Beyond the statutory minimum, Forest of Dean also runs a non-statutory sign-up alert service by area, and case officers may discretionarily notify additional neighbouring properties by flyer at the time of a site visit, assessed case by case depending on likely impact. Has adopted its own negotiation protocol, similar to Cotswold's.
