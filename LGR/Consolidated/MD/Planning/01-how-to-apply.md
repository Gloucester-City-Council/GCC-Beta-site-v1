# Applying for planning permission

<!-- common -->
## Do you need permission at all?

Some minor works — permitted development — don't need a planning application at all, under the Town and Country Planning (General Permitted Development) (England) Order 2015. Permitted development rights can be more restricted in a conservation area, for a listed building, or where a previous planning permission specifically removed them — always check the planning history of a property before assuming permitted development applies. Where permission genuinely isn't needed but the work still needs sign-off, that's handled through prior approval/notification instead (for things like larger home extensions, agricultural or forestry development, and some changes of use), or a Lawful Development Certificate if you want formal written proof the work is legal — for proposed work, or for work already carried out.

## Application types

- **Householder** — a simplified process for altering or enlarging a single house (not a flat), including garden/boundary works
- **Full application** — detailed proposals for anything not covered by householder rules or permitted development: new buildings, commercial property
- **Outline** — agreement in principle for how a site could be developed, followed later by a Reserved Matters application once outstanding details are settled (either "some matters reserved" or "all matters reserved")
- **Listed building consent**, **advertisement consent**, **demolition in a conservation area** — each has its own validation checklist
- **Non-material amendment** — for small changes after permission has already been granted
- **Removal or variation of a condition** / **discharge of conditions** — where permission was granted subject to conditions that need formally clearing or changing

## What you need to submit

A completed application form, the correct fee, site plans to scale (1:1250 or 1:2500), and whatever supporting information — "validation criteria" — applies to your application type. All plans and drawings need the paper size, site address, metric scale, correct title, and north point shown. The quickest route is the Planning Portal, which also has an online fee calculator; paper applications by post or hand delivery are accepted everywhere but generally take longer to process and usually attract a local administration fee (see the fees page).

Getting the validation checklist right matters more than it might seem — a high proportion of applications are returned as invalid purely because the site plan isn't to the correct scale, doesn't show the red-line boundary correctly, or supporting drawings are photographs rather than proper scaled plans. An invalid application typically adds 2–3 weeks before it's even properly logged.

## Pre-application advice

Every council offers a paid pre-application advice service, and getting advice before submitting is generally recommended — it can flag likely objections and reduce the risk of a formal refusal later.

<!-- council:gloucester -->
**Gloucester City Council.** Validation checklists available on the council's planning pages; standard process otherwise follows the common national pattern above.
<!-- /council:gloucester -->

<!-- council:stroud -->
**Stroud District Council.** Apply via the Planning Portal (pay the full fee there at submission) or by email to planning@stroud.gov.uk with the relevant downloaded form — Stroud asks for only one copy of the form and supporting documents per application type, except joint applications (e.g. listed building plus planning), which need two copies. Where a development needs a footpath or bridleway diversion or closure, permanent diversions go through Stroud District Council under the Town and Country Planning Act 1990 (a lengthy, not-always-successful process, paid separately from the planning application fee); temporary closures under 6 months, or any diversion unrelated to development, go through Gloucestershire County Council instead.
<!-- /council:stroud -->

<!-- council:cotswold -->
**Cotswold District Council.** Submission isn't restricted to the Planning Portal — paper forms can be downloaded from the Planning Portal and posted or hand-delivered. You'll need to check separately whether Community Infrastructure Levy applies and submit the CIL additional information form alongside your application if so.
<!-- /council:cotswold -->

<!-- council:forest-of-dean -->
**Forest of Dean District Council.** Online submission via the Planning Portal is recommended as the fastest route; paper forms can be posted or hand-delivered if you're unable to apply online. Forest of Dean has adopted a negotiation protocol setting out what amendments will be accepted during an application's lifetime — applicants and agents are encouraged to read it before submitting amended plans. Forest of Dean also runs a specific paid service to determine whether you need planning permission at all: submit basic sketch plans (dimensions in metres, not feet) and get a written decision within 28 days — useful evidence to keep, including when selling a property later.
<!-- /council:forest-of-dean -->

<!-- council:tewkesbury -->
**Tewkesbury Borough Council.** Contact: developmentapplications@tewkesbury.gov.uk, 01684 272151. Applying online via the Planning Portal (or the "PlanDA" portal) avoids the up-to-2-week delay that postal applications can add, since online applications reach the council immediately. Tewkesbury publishes a specific Householder Planning Permission Guide aimed at reducing invalid applications — the council receives around 600 householder applications a year, and roughly 40% fail validation at the first attempt. The most common reasons: a site location plan not drawn to the correct scale (1:1250/1:2500) or not showing the red-line boundary correctly, a block plan (1:200/1:500) that doesn't show the proposed development, elevations/floor plans not drawn to a recognised scale (1:50/1:100), or a photograph submitted instead of a proper scaled plan.
