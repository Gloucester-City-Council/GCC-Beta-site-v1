# Adult Social Care

Adult Social Care helps adults aged 18 and over stay independent, safe and well — whatever's behind the need, whether that's a learning disability, physical disability, mental health condition, dementia, autism, or another long-term condition. Support for carers, including young carers, is part of the same service.

Some people only need help for a short period — recovering after a hospital stay, for example. Others need care that builds up gradually, or support for life. The starting point is the same either way: talk to the council, and they'll work with you to understand what you actually need.

## What support looks like

- Information, advice and guidance, including for unpaid carers
- Help staying connected — friends, family, social activities
- Equipment, gadgets and home adaptations
- Personal care: washing, dressing, household tasks
- Supported living or a care home placement, where that's the right fit

## Getting an assessment

If you think you, or someone you care for, might need extra help, you can ask for a Care and Support Assessment. Gloucestershire calls this an "Adult Social Care Assessment" internally — same process, just a different name to the one used in some national guidance.

## Paying for care

Social care for adults is hardly ever free. Almost all care and support arranged or provided by the council is means-tested. A financial assessment works out what you'll pay. From there:

- **Self-funding** — if you don't qualify for council support, you pay for your own care
- **Deferred payments** — a way to delay paying certain care home costs, usually secured against your property
- **Direct payments** — money paid to you so you can arrange your own care
- **Personal budgets** — an agreed amount to spend on your care and support
- **Property disregards** — situations where your home isn't counted in the financial assessment, for example if a spouse or certain relatives still live there

A full "Paying for Adult Social Care" booklet is available, including easy read and large print versions.

## Keeping people safe

If you're worried a vulnerable adult is at risk of abuse or neglect, contact the Adult Social Care Helpdesk, or the police on 101 — 999 if it's an emergency. Professionals who suspect abuse or risk complete a safeguarding referral through the Adult Social Care portal. Where someone's freedom needs to be restricted for their own safety, usually in a care setting, that's handled through the Deprivation of Liberty Safeguards (DoLS) process.

## Getting in touch

- **Adult Social Care Helpdesk:** 01452 426868, Monday–Friday, 8am–5pm
- **Email:** socialcare.enq@gloucestershire.gov.uk
- **Emergency Duty Team** (out of hours, for serious social care emergencies including suspected abuse): via the council's emergency contacts page
- **Referrals and enquiries:** through the Helpdesk enquiry form, or the online Adult Social Care Portal

Therapy referrals — community nursing, occupational therapy, physiotherapy, reablement — go through Integrated Community Teams, who can also carry out a carer's assessment for anyone supporting someone with support needs.

## The legal framework

Adult social care in Gloucestershire operates under the Care Act 2014 and is regulated by the Care Quality Commission. The council's Your Circle directory signposts to local voluntary and community care options — being listed isn't an endorsement, so it's worth checking an organisation's own qualifications and inspection reports directly.
