Applying for planning permission

Whether you need permission, the application types and what to submit — with local submission notes for your area.

← All planning topics

Do you need permission at all?

Some minor works — permitted development — don't need a planning application at all, under the Town and Country Planning (General Permitted Development) (England) Order 2015. These rights can be more restricted in a conservation area, for a listed building, or where a previous permission specifically removed them — always check a property's planning history before assuming permitted development applies. Where permission isn't needed but the work still needs sign-off, that's handled through prior approval/notification, or a Lawful Development Certificate if you want formal written proof the work is legal (for proposed or already-completed work).

Application types

  • Householder — a simplified process for altering or enlarging a single house (not a flat), including garden/boundary works
  • Full application — detailed proposals for anything not covered by householder rules or permitted development: new buildings, commercial property
  • Outline — agreement in principle for how a site could be developed, followed by a Reserved Matters application once details are settled
  • Listed building consent, advertisement consent, demolition in a conservation area — each has its own validation checklist
  • Non-material amendment — for small changes after permission has been granted
  • Removal or variation of a condition / discharge of conditions — to formally clear or change conditions attached to a permission

What you need to submit

A completed application form, the correct fee, site plans to scale (1:1250 or 1:2500), and whatever supporting "validation criteria" applies to your application type. All plans need the paper size, site address, metric scale, correct title and north point shown. The quickest route is the Planning Portal, which has an online fee calculator; paper applications by post or hand delivery are accepted everywhere but generally take longer and usually attract a local administration fee (see the fees page).

Getting the validation checklist right matters: a high proportion of applications are returned as invalid purely because the site plan isn't to the correct scale, doesn't show the red-line boundary correctly, or supporting drawings are photographs rather than proper scaled plans. An invalid application typically adds 2–3 weeks before it's even logged.

Pre-application advice

Every council offers a paid pre-application advice service, and getting advice before submitting is generally recommended — it can flag likely objections and reduce the risk of a formal refusal later.

Gloucester City Council

Validation checklists are available on the council's planning pages; the process otherwise follows the common national pattern above.

Stroud District Council

Apply via the Planning Portal (pay the full fee there at submission) or by email to planning@stroud.gov.uk with the relevant downloaded form — Stroud asks for only one copy of the form and supporting documents per application type, except joint applications (e.g. listed building plus planning), which need two copies.

Where a development needs a footpath or bridleway diversion or closure, permanent diversions go through Stroud District Council under the Town and Country Planning Act 1990 (a lengthy process, paid separately from the application fee); temporary closures under 6 months, or any diversion unrelated to development, go through Gloucestershire County Council instead.

Cotswold District Council

Submission isn't restricted to the Planning Portal — paper forms can be downloaded from the Portal and posted or hand-delivered. You'll need to check separately whether Community Infrastructure Levy applies and submit the CIL additional information form alongside your application if so.

Forest of Dean District Council

Online submission via the Planning Portal is recommended as the fastest route; paper forms can be posted or hand-delivered if you can't apply online. Forest of Dean has a negotiation protocol setting out what amendments will be accepted during an application's lifetime — read it before submitting amended plans.

It also runs a paid service to determine whether you need permission at all: submit basic sketch plans (dimensions in metres) and get a written decision within 28 days — useful evidence to keep, including when selling a property later.

Tewkesbury Borough Council

Contact: developmentapplications@tewkesbury.gov.uk, 01684 272151. Applying online via the Planning Portal (or the "PlanDA" portal) avoids the up-to-2-week delay postal applications can add.

Tewkesbury publishes a Householder Planning Permission Guide aimed at reducing invalid applications — it receives around 600 householder applications a year, roughly 40% of which fail validation at the first attempt. Common reasons: a site location plan not to the correct scale (1:1250/1:2500) or not showing the red-line boundary, a block plan (1:200/1:500) that doesn't show the proposed development, elevations/floor plans not to a recognised scale (1:50/1:100), or a photograph submitted instead of a proper scaled plan.
