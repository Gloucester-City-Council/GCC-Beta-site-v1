Council tax exemptions

Some properties are exempt from council tax altogether — no charge, not a discount. If a property meets one of the conditions below, the bill is nil while that condition applies.

This list is set in national legislation. It is the same in every council area; nobody locally chooses which classes apply.

Unoccupied properties

  • Owned by a charity — empty for less than 6 months since the last occupation, and was last occupied for the charity's purposes.
  • Left empty by someone now in prison or detained elsewhere — would otherwise be their home.
  • Left empty by someone now in a hospital, care home or hostel — was previously their main home.
  • Awaiting probate — empty since the owner's death, no executor or administrator liable other than in that capacity, and (where probate-dependent) less than 6 months since probate or letters of administration were granted.
  • Occupation prohibited by law — a planning condition or other legal restriction prevents anyone living there.
  • Held for a minister of religion — kept as a future residence for clergy.
  • Owner now receiving personal care elsewhere — for example due to old age, illness, or disability, and was previously their main home.
  • Owner now living elsewhere to provide personal care to someone else.
  • Left empty by a student — was last occupied by someone who has since become a student, or who was already a student.
  • Mortgagee in possession.
  • Student hall of residence.
  • Armed forces accommodation (excluding visiting forces accommodation, which has its own rule).
  • Visiting forces members.
  • Trustee in bankruptcy holds the property.
  • Unoccupied caravan pitch or boat mooring.
  • Unoccupied annexe that can't legally be let separately from the main house.

Occupied properties

  • Everyone living there is under 18.
  • Everyone living there is a student, or a student's qualifying dependant, or a school/college leaver, plus some Ukraine-scheme arrangements.
  • Everyone living there is severely mentally impaired (or SMI residents plus students/qualifying dependants).
  • A diplomat or someone with equivalent immunity lives there, and it isn't their main UK residence, and they're not a British national or permanent resident.
  • A dependent relative's annexe, where the relative lives in the annexe and a family member lives in the main house.
Note: Classes A and C (short-term empty-and-unfurnished, and empty-undergoing-repair) were removed in England from 1 April 2013. The long-term empty premium rules now cover that ground instead.

Council tax discounts

Single person discount

If you're the only adult living in the property, the bill is reduced by 25%. This is a national entitlement, set the same way everywhere — it isn't a local decision.

Properties left empty and unfurnished

The default position in law is a 50% discount when nobody lives in a property. Councils have the power to reduce that discount, including down to nothing, for furnished-but-unoccupied and unfurnished-and-unoccupied properties (the s.11A power). Most councils have used that power. What's below is what your council has actually set.

Unoccupied and unfurnished: 25% discount for up to 6 months from when the furniture was removed. After 6 months, full charge applies — no discount.

Unoccupied and unfurnished: 25% discount for up to 6 months. Full charge applies after that.

Major repair or structural work

A property that needs major repair or structural alteration to be habitable can qualify for a discount for up to 12 months while uninhabitable. What counts as "major" is set in law — roof structure repairs, rebuilding external walls or chimney stacks, foundation repair, replacing defective floor or ceiling joists, gutting most internal walls, or repairs after flood, fire or subsidence.

Kitchen/bathroom refurbishment, general repairs, redecoration, rewiring on its own, and damp-proofing do not qualify.

Gloucester's major repair discount is 25%, for up to 12 months.

Stroud's major repair discount (Class D) is 25%, for up to 12 months.

Long-term empty homes premium

A property that's been empty and substantially unfurnished for a long time can be charged an extra premium on top of the normal bill, to push owners toward bringing it back into use. The premium is attached to the property, not the owner — if you buy or rent a property that's already attracted the premium, you inherit the charge.

Maximum premiums allowed in law

  • Empty 1–5 years: up to 100% extra (double the normal bill)
  • Empty 5–10 years: up to 200% extra (triple)
  • Empty 10+ years: up to 300% extra (quadruple)

Whether a council has adopted these maximums, and from what date, is a local decision.

Gloucester City Council

  • From 1 April 2025: 100% premium once empty 1 year or more (previously the threshold was 2 years, set from 1 April 2020)
  • 200% once empty 5 years or more (from 1 April 2020)
  • 300% once empty 10 years or more (from 1 April 2021)

Decision taken at full council, 7 February 2024.

Stroud District Council

  • From 1 April 2025: 100% premium once empty 1 year or more (previously 2 years, set from 1 April 2020)
  • 200% once empty 5 years or more
  • 300% once empty 10 years or more

Decision taken at full council, 22 February 2024. Note: the premium clock attaches to the property — it does not reset on a change of owner or tenant.

Cotswold District Council

  • From 1 April 2024: 100% premium for 1–5 years empty

Exceptions — these properties don't get the premium

The same exception classes apply in every council area:

  • Class E — would be someone's main home if they weren't living in job-related armed forces accommodation
  • Class F — an annexe forming part of the main dwelling
  • Class G — actively marketed for sale (12-month limit)
  • Class H — actively marketed for let (12-month limit; from 1 May 2026 this expressly includes assured tenancies)
  • Class I — probate recently granted on a previously-exempt property (12 months from grant)
  • Class M — undergoing major repair or structural alteration (12-month limit)

After the exception period ends, the property falls back into the premium unless a fresh qualifying transaction resets the clock.

Second homes

A property that's furnished but isn't anyone's main home — used for weekends, holidays, or occasionally — is a second home. No discount applies to a second home; full council tax is payable as a baseline.

The Levelling-up and Regeneration Act 2023 gave councils the power to charge an additional premium on second homes, on top of the full charge. All five Gloucestershire councils have adopted a 100% premium from 1 April 2025; the difference is the date each resolved to do so.

Exceptions — these don't get the second homes premium

  • Class E — armed forces accommodation
  • Class F — occupied annexe
  • Class G — marketed for sale (12-month limit)
  • Class H — marketed for let (12-month limit; includes assured tenancies from 1 May 2026)
  • Class I — probate recently granted (12 months from grant)
  • Class J — job-related second home
  • Class K — caravan pitch or boat mooring
  • Class L — planning condition preventing occupation for 28+ continuous days, or restricting use to holiday letting, or barring use as a sole/main residence
If you believe a property is actually a holiday let rather than a second home, council tax still applies at the normal rate until the Valuation Office Agency agrees to move it onto the business rates list.

Council Tax Reduction (CTR)

Council Tax Reduction lowers the bill for people on a low income. It's deducted after any discounts and premiums have already been applied — it's the last adjustment before the demand notice is issued.

Pensioner route

If you've reached state pension credit qualifying age, you're assessed under the national pensioner scheme. This is set centrally — there's no local variation. Personal allowances, premiums (severe disability, carer, disabled child, enhanced disability) and the capital limit (£16,000) are fixed in regulations and identical across every council in England.

The calculation: if your income is below your applicable amount, you get maximum reduction. If it's above, the reduction tapers off — maximum reduction minus 20% of the daily excess. There's also an "alternative maximum" route based on a second adult's circumstances in the household, which is used if it produces a higher reduction than the income-based calculation.

Working-age route

Working-age applicants are assessed under each council's local scheme. The structure is built on a national Default Scheme template, but councils can vary the parameters within it — the taper rate, income bands, minimum payment requirements, and so on.

Applying and reporting changes

A change of circumstances that might affect your entitlement has to be reported within 21 days of it happening — this is a national rule (SI 2012/2885 Schedule 8 paragraph 9), not a local one. Failing to report can lead to a civil penalty.
