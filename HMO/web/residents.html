Living in an HMO — your rights and what to expect

Find out about your rights, the standards your landlord must meet, and what to do if there are problems.

What is an HMO? Your rights Licensing Reporting problems

What is an HMO?

A House in Multiple Occupation (HMO) is a property occupied by three or more people from two or more households who share facilities such as a kitchen, bathroom or toilet. (Housing Act 2004, sections 254–260)

Types of HMO

There are four main types of HMO:

  1. Bedsits — self-contained rooms where occupants share some or all facilities such as bathrooms and kitchens.
  2. Shared houses or flats — a property let to a group of people who are not related, sharing kitchen, bathroom and living facilities.
  3. Lodgings — a property where a landlord lives and rents rooms to three or more lodgers who are not related to the owner.
  4. Hostels and bed and breakfast accommodation — where the main use is not a hotel or guest house and the accommodation provides the main or only residence for occupants.

Your rights

As a tenant in an HMO you have a number of important legal rights. These include protections introduced by recent legislation.

  • Right to periodic assured tenancy — you cannot be locked into a fixed-term tenancy from 1 May 2026. (Renters' Rights Act 2025, ss.1–7)
  • Protection from no-fault eviction — Section 21 "no-fault" evictions have been abolished. Your landlord must have a valid reason to end your tenancy. (Renters' Rights Act 2025, s.8)
  • Rent increase limits — rent can only be increased once per 12 months. (Renters' Rights Act 2025, ss.124–130)
  • Tenancy deposit cap — your deposit is capped at 5 weeks' rent. (Tenant Fees Act 2019)
  • Protection from fees — most fees charged by landlords or agents are prohibited. (Tenant Fees Act 2019)
  • Rent Repayment Order — you may be able to apply to the First-tier Tribunal for a Rent Repayment Order of up to 12 months' rent where your HMO is unlicensed. (Housing and Planning Act 2016, ss.40–41)
  • Access to information about your landlord — you have the right to access information about your landlord on the Private Rented Sector (PRS) Database. (Renters' Rights Act 2025, ss.56–82)
  • Right to complain to the Landlord Ombudsman — all private landlords must belong to a landlord ombudsman scheme. (Renters' Rights Act 2025, ss.83–99)
  • Protection from discrimination — landlords and agents cannot discriminate against tenants with children or those in receipt of benefits. (Renters' Rights Act 2025, ss.131–135)

Licensing — what your landlord must do

Your landlord must have a licence for all HMO properties which are occupied by five or more people forming two or more households.

What is not an HMO?

The following are not classified as HMOs:

  • An entire flat or house let to one person, a couple, a family or two people sharing.
  • Two lodgers with an owner occupier living in the house.

Important: If your HMO is unlicensed you may be able to apply to the First-tier Tribunal for a Rent Repayment Order (up to 12 months' rent). [Housing and Planning Act 2016, ss.40–41]

Reporting problems

The council is responsible for enforcing HMO standards, so if you are a tenant living in a HMO and feel there are any unreasonable hazards which your private landlord is not dealing with, please report it.

The council must maintain a public register of all the premises licensed as a house in multiple occupation. You can contact us to check whether a property is registered.

Your duties as a tenant

The management regulations also place a duty on tenants not to hinder managers in complying with these duties and to provide necessary information to help comply with these duties. You must:

  • Take reasonable care not to cause any damage to anything the manager has provided
  • Store and dispose of refuse appropriately
  • Comply with reasonable instructions regarding fire safety and means of escape

Real situations — what to do

My landlord wants to raise the rent. Can they do it whenever they want?

No. Rent can only be increased once per 12 months via a statutory process. Your landlord must give you formal notice and follow legal procedures. If they try to increase rent more frequently or outside the legal process, this is a breach of your rights. Contact the council if you believe your landlord is breaching these protections.

Contact: 01452 396 396 or heretohelp@gloucester.gov.uk

The heating is broken and my landlord says they'll fix it in a month. What can I do?

Your landlord must maintain the property to required standards, including essential services like heating. If they are not dealing with unreasonable hazards or repairs, you can report this to the council. We are responsible for enforcing HMO standards and can take action against landlords who fail to meet legal requirements.

Report a problem:

  • Phone: 01452 396 396
  • Online: Report a problem
I got an eviction notice. What are my rights?

Your landlord cannot use "no-fault" evictions. They must have a valid legal reason to end your tenancy and must follow proper legal processes. From 1 May 2026, you also cannot be locked into a fixed-term tenancy — all tenancies are periodic, giving you more flexibility.

If you receive an eviction notice, seek legal advice immediately. Contact the council or a tenant advice service for guidance on your rights and what to do next.

Contact: 01452 396 396

My landlord is keeping some of my deposit. Is this legal?

Your deposit is capped at 5 weeks' rent. Your landlord can only deduct from your deposit for legitimate reasons (damage beyond normal wear and tear, unpaid rent, etc.) and must follow proper procedures. They cannot use your deposit to cover repairs that are their legal responsibility.

If you believe your deposit is being wrongly withheld, contact the council. You may also be able to apply to the First-tier Tribunal for a Rent Repayment Order if the deposit breach is part of a broader unlicensed HMO issue.

Contact: 01452 396 396 or heretohelp@gloucester.gov.uk
