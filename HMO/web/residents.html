I'm a resident or tenant

Understand your rights, know what standards your landlord must meet, and learn how to report problems.

What is an HMO?

A House in Multiple Occupation (HMO) is a property occupied by three or more people from two or more households who share facilities such as a kitchen, bathroom or toilet. (Housing Act 2004, sections 254–260)

Understanding households: A household is a group of people who share cooking facilities and meals together.

  • Partners sharing a room: 1 household
  • Unrelated occupants: separate households

What is not an HMO?

The following are not classified as HMOs:

  • A self-contained flat or house occupied by one person, a couple, a single family, or two individuals sharing.
  • A property where up to two lodgers live with the owner-occupier.

Does your property need a licence?

⚠️

Licence required

5 or more people from 2 or more separate households

✓

No licence needed

Fewer than 5 people, or just 1 household

If your HMO is unlicensed: You may be able to apply to the First-tier Tribunal for a Rent Repayment Order (up to 12 months' rent). (Housing and Planning Act 2016, ss.40–41)

Your rights

As a tenant in an HMO you have a number of important legal rights. These include protections introduced by recent legislation.

  • Right to periodic assured tenancy — you cannot be locked into a fixed-term tenancy from 1 May 2026. (Renters' Rights Act 2025, ss.1–7)
  • Protection from no-fault eviction — Section 21 "no-fault" evictions have been abolished. Your landlord must have a valid reason to end your tenancy. (Renters' Rights Act 2025, s.8)
  • Rent increase limits — rent can only be increased once per 12 months. (Renters' Rights Act 2025, ss.124–130)
  • Tenancy deposit cap — your deposit is capped at 5 weeks' rent. (Tenant Fees Act 2019)
  • Protection from fees — most fees charged by landlords or agents are prohibited. (Tenant Fees Act 2019)
  • Rent Repayment Order — you may be able to apply to the First-tier Tribunal for a Rent Repayment Order of up to 12 months' rent where your HMO is unlicensed. (Housing and Planning Act 2016, ss.40–41)
  • Access to information about your landlord — you have the right to access information about your landlord on the Private Rented Sector (PRS) Database. (Renters' Rights Act 2025, ss.56–82)
  • Right to complain to the Landlord Ombudsman — all private landlords must belong to a landlord ombudsman scheme. (Renters' Rights Act 2025, ss.83–99)
  • Protection from discrimination — landlords and agents cannot discriminate against tenants with children or those in receipt of benefits. (Renters' Rights Act 2025, ss.131–135)

Housing standards and safety

Your landlord must provide and maintain safe housing that meets minimum standards. Below are the key areas they must comply with.

Fire safety

Your landlord must ensure the property meets fire safety standards:

  • Interlinked smoke detectors fitted in hallways and on landings
  • Heat detector fitted in the kitchen
  • 30-minute fire-resistant escape routes with no obstructions
  • Self-closing fire doors on bedrooms, kitchens and living rooms (if 3 or more storeys)
  • Fire blankets in rooms with cooking facilities
  • Emergency lighting in properties with 3 or more storeys
  • Regular fire risk assessments and reviews

Essential services

Your landlord must provide and maintain:

  • Heating and hot water — available year-round in adequate supply
  • Safe gas installations — annual Gas Safety Certificate (CP12) required for all gas appliances
  • Safe electrical installations — Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR) required every 5 years
  • Clean water supply — mains connection with adequate pressure and flow
  • Working drainage — properly functioning and maintained

Space and facilities

Your landlord must provide adequate space and facilities:

  • Bedroom sizes — minimum 6.5 sqm for one person with shared facilities; 12.5 sqm for one person with own cooking facilities
  • Communal space — adequate kitchen and living areas based on number of occupants
  • Bathroom and toilet provision — one bathroom for up to 5 people; additional facilities for more occupants
  • Hot and cold water — supplied to all baths, showers and washbasins

Property maintenance

Your landlord must:

  • Keep common areas clean and in good decorative repair
  • Maintain all fixtures, fittings and appliances in safe working condition
  • Keep escape routes clear and free from obstruction
  • Carry out regular maintenance and repairs

Health and safety standards

The council uses the Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS) to assess hazards in HMOs. This includes 29 hazard groups such as damp, excess cold, electrical hazards, falls, fire risks and many others.

For detailed information about these standards, see: HMO standards and safety requirements

If there's a problem

If you notice hazards or your landlord isn't maintaining the property to required standards, follow these steps:

Step 1: Document the issue

  • Take photos or video of the problem
  • Note the date and what's wrong
  • Keep records of any communication with your landlord

Step 2: Contact your landlord

  • Send an email or letter describing the problem
  • Request they fix it within a reasonable timeframe (7–14 days depending on severity)
  • Keep a copy of your communication

Step 3: If they don't respond or refuse

  • Contact the council to report the hazard
  • Provide your photos/evidence and copies of your communication with the landlord
  • The council is responsible for enforcing HMO standards

What happens next

When you report a problem to the council:

  • The council will investigate the hazard and assess its severity
  • They may first work informally with the landlord to agree remedial works
  • If informal action doesn't work, the council may serve an Improvement Notice requiring repairs within a set timeframe
  • For serious hazards, they may issue a Prohibition Order restricting use of the property until hazards are fixed
  • Non-compliance can result in enforcement action and fines

The council maintains a public register of all premises licensed as a house in multiple occupation. You can contact them to check whether your property is registered.

What you must do

As a resident, you also have responsibilities. The management regulations place duties on tenants to cooperate with their landlord or manager.

Cooperate with your landlord and the council

  • Allow access for necessary inspections and repairs
  • Respond promptly to requests for information
  • Don't hinder your landlord or manager in carrying out their legal duties

Maintain the property

  • Take reasonable care not to cause damage to anything the manager has provided
  • Store and dispose of refuse appropriately
  • Keep your space reasonably clean and hygienic

Follow fire safety procedures

  • Comply with fire safety instructions and evacuation procedures
  • Keep escape routes clear and unobstructed
  • Don't disable or tamper with fire safety equipment

Report hazards promptly

If you notice problems with the property, report them to your landlord and/or the council. Don't ignore hazards that could affect your safety or health.
