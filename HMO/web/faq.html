HMO frequently asked questions

Answers to the most common questions about Houses in Multiple Occupation in Gloucester. Cannot find what you are looking for? Contact us.

For residents and tenants

Information for people who live or are thinking of living in an HMO in Gloucester.

Does my landlord need an HMO licence?

Your landlord must have a licence for all HMO properties which are occupied by five or more people forming two or more households. If you think your landlord is operating unlicensed, contact the council on 01452 396 396.

See also: HMO licensing information.

What is an HMO?

A HMO is a house in multiple occupation and applies to buildings that are either a single dwelling or a flat where one or more separate households share facilities such as; kitchens, toilet and bathroom.

See also: Living in an HMO — your rights and what to expect.

What is not an HMO?

The following are not classified as HMOs:

  • An entire flat or house let to one person, a couple, a family or two people sharing.
  • Two lodgers with an owner occupier living in the house.
What can I do if my landlord won't fix hazards?

The council is responsible for enforcing HMO standards, so if you are a tenant living in a HMO and feel there are any unreasonable hazards which your private landlord is not dealing with, please report it.

Contact us:

  • Phone: 01452 396 396
  • Email: heretohelp@gloucester.gov.uk
Can I claim back rent if my HMO is unlicensed?

You may be able to apply to the First-tier Tribunal for a Rent Repayment Order of up to 12 months' rent where an HMO is unlicensed. (Housing and Planning Act 2016, ss.40–41)

Contact the council on 01452 396 396 for advice on how to proceed.

For landlords and managers

Information for people who own or manage Houses in Multiple Occupation in Gloucester.

Do I need a licence?

A licence is required for all HMOs that are occupied by five or more people belonging to 2 or more households and who share a facility or amenity.

See also: HMO licensing in Gloucester.

How much does a licence cost?

Fees from 1 April 2026:

  • New licence (up to 5 years): £1,260.00
  • Renewal (up to 5 years): £900.00

See also: Apply for an HMO licence.

How long does an HMO licence last?

The licence is valid for a period of up to five years. New applications are dated from when the application is deemed valid.

Do I need planning permission?

Planning and licensing are two different matters. You may not need planning permission but could still need a licence from the Council to operate. HMOs with five or more people require a licence.

  • Small HMOs (3–6 people, use class C4): Typically do not need planning permission, as a permitted development right applies for change of use from C3 to C4.
  • Large HMOs (7 or more people, Sui Generis): Always require planning permission in addition to a licence.

See also: Planning permission for HMOs.

What happens if I operate without a licence?

Operating an unlicensed HMO is a criminal offence carrying an unlimited fine on conviction, or a civil penalty of up to £30,000. (Housing Act 2004, s.72; Housing and Planning Act 2016, ss.28–31)

Important: Tenants in an unlicensed HMO may also apply to the First-tier Tribunal for a Rent Repayment Order of up to 12 months' rent. (Housing and Planning Act 2016, ss.40–41)

What fire safety is required?

As a minimum, interlinked smoke detectors must be fitted in hallways and on landings, and a heat detector must be fitted in the kitchen. These must comply with BS 5839:2019 part 6 — Grade D:LD2.

Specific requirements depend on the number of storeys and layout of the property. Three-storey or taller properties have additional requirements including 30-minute fire doors and emergency lighting.

See also: HMO standards and safety requirements.

What are the minimum bedroom sizes?

Rooms with shared facilities:

  • 1 person: 6.5 sqm
  • 2 persons: 10.2 sqm

Rooms with cooking facilities:

  • 1 person: 12.5 sqm
  • 2 persons: 15.5 sqm

See also: HMO standards and safety requirements.

Enforcement and complaints

Information about enforcement action and how the council handles HMO complaints.

What enforcement powers does the council have?

The council can take a range of enforcement action where an HMO does not meet required standards or where a property is operated without a licence:

  • Improvement Notices — requiring specified works to be carried out within a set timescale. (Housing Act 2004)
  • Prohibition Orders — prohibiting the use of part or all of a property, or restricting the number of occupants. (Housing Act 2004)
  • Civil penalties — of up to £30,000 per offence. (Housing and Planning Act 2016)
  • Banning orders — in serious cases, preventing a landlord from letting or managing properties. (Housing and Planning Act 2016)
  • Management orders — in serious cases, taking over the management of a property. (Housing Act 2004, ss.101–131)
Can the council take over management of my HMO?

In cases where an HMO is unlicensed and a suitable licence holder cannot be found, or where licensable conditions persistently cannot be met, the council may apply for an Interim or Final Management Order taking over management of the property. (Housing Act 2004, ss.101–131)

The council will use management orders only as a last resort, after other enforcement options have been exhausted.
